A concrete question about finite observers

Finite Directional Records as a Concrete Observer Model

Can one localized system acquire, retain, and expose a relational orientation record while its information, support, energy cost, and gravitational footprint are controlled in one semiclassical regime?

The observer-level question

Observer rules in quantum gravity assign finite operational content to an observer subsystem, while de Sitter observer algebras make the observer itself part of the construction [1]-[4]. We ask whether a deliberately narrow physical register can make part of that abstraction auditable: a localized system that records the relative orientation of a second localized system, stores the record for a stated proper time, and permits a later relational readout.

The aim is not to identify a rotor with an observer. It is to test whether a sharply defined candidate record resource can expose which information, dynamics, localization, and gravitational inputs an observer rule actually needs.

Why direction is useful

Finite spatial reference frames and their degradation are established subjects [5]-[7]. Direction is useful here because the task can be wholly relational. If O is the register and D the target, the unknown datum is their alignment, not either orientation relative to coordinates:

g_rel = g_O^(-1) g_D in SO(3). (1)

Performance is the Haar-prior global risk for estimating g_rel. This probes the full rotational orbit and distinguishes a useful record from a large but rotationally invariant Hilbert space.

The record variable

For the covariant orientation ensemble, we begin with the relative entropy of rotational asymmetry:

S_dir := D(rho_O || G_SO(3)[rho_O])
= S(G_SO(3)[rho_O]) - S(rho_O). (2)

Here G_SO(3) is rotational twirling. For this task, S_dir is an operational rotational-asymmetry resource. It is not identified here with an effective classical record dimension, thermodynamic entropy, Hilbert-space dimension, Casimir, quantum Fisher information, or Harlow's S_Ob. Whether any comparison with S_Ob is meaningful is the first review question.

Lifetime is operational too: after storage time T, an allowed relational readout must still attain a declared risk. This is stability of a classical record, not generic quantum phase coherence.

The proposed theorem chain

Fix a declared class C_dir of localized register-target models, states, environments, local actions, and preparation/storage/readout protocols. The intended theorem quantifies over that class:

R_rel(T) >= F_record(S_dir, degradation, channel error)
S_dir <= F_capacity(energy, support, gravity; model data)
=> Phi(R_rel(T), T, support, gravity; model data) >= 0. (3)

Several arrows are rigorous on named domains: global risk requires rotational asymmetry and angular-momentum resources; confined orbital matter converts angular resource into support and excitation-energy cost; and a stipulated isotropic heat channel gives a conditional finite-time degradation law.

The primary bottleneck

The common-action physical record channel is open. One local action must justify acquisition, storage degradation, and readout; control the error between microscopic KMS dynamics and the effective record channel; and enter the same stress-energy ledger. De Sitter master equations and controlled Markovian approximations show what must be specified, not that this bridge follows from thermality alone [10].

What gravity and the realization add

On fixed de Sitter, exact source, transmission, and electric-Weyl reconstruction maps provide a gauge-invariant route from a conserved quadrupolar stress to an exterior witness [11]. No rigorous continuum interval for the completed source presently excludes zero. Even after zero exclusion, this fixed-background witness is not a capacity bound; collapse, horizon or quantum-extremal-surface displacement, or another invariant may be the relevant constraint.

A supported massive Skyrmion supplies one certified local-matter candidate, against a literature where curved and gravitating de Sitter Skyrmions already exist [12]. It demonstrates that the supported-profile subproblem is nonempty; the observer architecture must survive rejection of its hard wall, membrane, or field model.

Why request feedback now

The project is at a fork before its most expensive bridge is built. We seek conceptual direction on the S_dir-to-S_Ob dictionary, the gravitational quantity that should constrain a finite register, and whether a one-action semiclassical record model clarifies the observer rule without beginning from a closed-universe path integral. This is a request for criticism and redirection, not endorsement or a publication decision.

Claim boundary: S_dir != S_Ob; record stability is not quantum coherence; measurement back-action is not gravitational backreaction; fixed-background response is not self-consistent gravity.

Where the observer model should connect to quantum gravity

These questions are intentionally upstream of the remaining interval calculation. A negative answer to any one of them would redirect the architecture while that redirection is still inexpensive.

  1. Is S_dir, used here as an operational SO(3)-asymmetry resource for a covariant alignment task, meaningfully comparable to the observer entropy S_Ob in the observer rule, or are these fundamentally different resources?
  2. Which gauge-invariant gravitational quantity should constrain the capacity of a finite observer register in a de Sitter causal patch: collapse, horizon or quantum-extremal-surface displacement, exterior response, or something else?
  3. Would a one-action semiclassical model deriving relational record formation, lifetime, localization, readout, and gravitational footprint clarify the observer rule, or is a closed-universe gravitational path integral essential from the outset?
Feedback target. We are not asking whether the present realization is complete. We are asking whether this is the right operational bridge to build, which quantity should replace the provisional gravitational witness if needed, and which observer-level identification should remain deliberately absent.

B. Capacity from localization and energy

[PROVED] For finitely many spinless nonrelativistic particles of total mass M, hard support |x_i|<=a, and a ground-subtracted excitation Hamiltonian satisfying the quadratic-form inequality H_ex>=sum_i p_i^2/(2m_i), every form-domain state obeys

<L^2> <= 2 M a^2 E_ex,
R_ref >= 1/(32 M a^2 E_ex+8). (B.1)

The result includes mixed, zero-mean, and rare-tail states with rotation-trivial internal labels. It excludes intrinsic spin, relativistic or massless fields, soft localization, negative interaction energy, and nontrivially rotating light species. A separate compactness substitution is a declared proxy, not a general-relativistic body theorem. See W3a and docs/localized_orbital_reference.md.

[PROVED] More generally, sector floors epsilon_j yield S_dir<=beta E+log Z_H(beta) only when Z_H(beta)=sum_j(2j+1)^2 exp(-beta epsilon_j) is finite. Covariance plus finite mean energy is insufficient; a bounded invariant counterexample permits increasingly accurate frame states at uniformly bounded energy. A physical theorem must derive growing sector floors from its matter model.

[PROVED] In the hard-supported massive-Skyrme hedgehog collective family on fixed de Sitter, with support radius a and minimum support lapse N_w>0, the static energy and collective inertia obey

I[F] <= 4 M[F] a^2/(3N_w),
E_j >= sqrt[3N_w j(j+1)/2]/a. (B.2)

This produces a linear large-spin floor and finite integer or projective rotational partition function. It is uniform over radial relaxation inside the adiabatic collective family (W3b).

[CONDITIONAL] The Skyrmion is therefore a concrete candidate for the capacity premise, not a complete rotating-field observer. Collective-band completeness, noncollective modes, projection error, right/isospin access, and the capacity of a marked rotating wall remain uncontrolled.
